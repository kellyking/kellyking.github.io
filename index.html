problem

No one was using the main navigation on the website. Customers were using search as their primary means of locating products and information.

A navigation should help users quickly find the product they are looking for by immediately narrowing the catalog. By defaulting to search, users were presented with a large search result list that they then had to filter themselves. In addition to the main navigation problem, the filters were not being used on the search results page, but that is another story.

hypothesis

Working with the design manager and product manager, we hypothesized that the reason why no one was using the manin navigation was because the link text was too specific, confusing, and there were too many choices to choose from (11 links).

We decided we would come up with new navigation links that would help users quickly find what they were looking for.

data to the rescue

We use a third-party service, Nextopia, to power our search on the website. I downloaded at the search terms from the beginning of 2019, and categorized the top 500 terms. I labeled each one: author, topic, product title, the word 'free', podcast, blog, anything relating to customer service, digital library, etc.

The results showed that the majority (44.8%) of users are searching for a specific author name. The next highest was topic (), followed by product title (). The rest of the search terms were below 3% each.

proposed solution

Working with the design manager and product manager, we took this data and created new navigation links. We ended up created two versions of the navigation, since there was internal disagreement about some links.

research

I completed in-person prototype testing of the two navigations. My findings were:

  • Participants were confused by the term 'Event' and described products completely dissimilar to our event products.
  • Including the term 'Topics' made it easier for some participants to complete their tasks.
  • The term 'Topics' helped the participants learn about the company.
  • Participants immediately knew we published books because of the term 'Authors'.
  • Including the terms 'Podcast' and 'Blog' made it easier for participants to complete thier tasks that were related to these.
  • Participants were unclear what type of offerings 'Online Courses' were.

After reviewing the tests, I created a new version of the navigation which had the following terms: Shop, Authors, Topics, Online Learning, Events, Blog, Podcast, Contact Us. I also communicated the findings regarding the term 'Events' to the Events team.

The Events team discussed the problem with the term 'Events' and proposed a new term: 'Trainings & Retreats'. I was initally unsure of this term. Firstly, I felt it was too long for an itme in the main navigation. Secondly, I wasn't sure that users would understand the term.

I created a new version of the navigation which had the following terms: Shop, Authors, Topics, Online Learning, Trainings & Retreats, Blog, Podcast, Contact Us. I did another round of paper prototype tests.

My findings were:

  • Participants did understand the term 'Trainings & Retreats'. They described products that were similar to our training and retreat products.

solution

These are wireframes of the final navigation:

I removed the word 'Retreats' from the term 'Trainings & Retreats' for two reasons. First, it made the term too long. Second, we only produce a few retreats a year and including it in the main navigation seemed insincere.

digital library pwa

summary

The Digital Library is what customers use to comsume the media they purchase from us. For example, someone purchases "Guided Mindfulness Meditation" audio download. In their Digital Library, they can listen to the product and download all of its files. We are replacing our native Digital Libray apps and the Digital Library section of our website with a PWA (progressive web app).

problem

How are our customers currently using the Digital Library? What problems are they having? What functionality does the new pwa need to have? What user flows does it need to provide? These are all things that need to be considered for the creation of the new pwa.

data to the rescue

Fortunately, we have several hundred app reviews for both our iOS and Android apps. I read through the reviews and compiled lists of:

  • functionality that is working
  • new functionality that customers are asking for
  • user flows were customers are getting stuck or confused

Overall, customers were intially happy when the apps first came out. However, due to the lack of regular updates and enhancements, they were now disappointed in the apps' lack of features that are commonally found in similar applications, such as iTunes, Netflix, Amazon, eReaders, etc.

proposed solution

The need to ship a MVP quickly has restricted the list of features that are initially being implemented. The MVP will be a parity of the current experience, with a few of the glaring issues resolved.

In future releases, we will be including these new features that customers had requested in their reviews: bookmarking within a media file, creating custom playlists, ten-second rewind and fast-forward buttons, closed captions, transcripts, ability to read an eBook withing the pwa, keyword search, offering similar products by the same author/genre.
