Product Sales Page

skills

  • Workshops
  • Journey Asset Map
  • Quantitative data analysis
  • UX best-practices

summary

Lead a workshop to create Journey Asset Map of the marketing assets for a mindfulness online course. Analyze the results and make recommendations for improvement.

problem

We actively market this product several times a year, and have done so for the past four years. Sales had platued. The sell page included an immense amount of information and as a result was extremely long and unfocused.

data to the rescue

I worked with the Project Manager, Design Manager and Copywriter to create a Journey Asset Map of the product. We printed out all visual assets for the product, tapped them to a large wall, and mapped out the multiple customer journeys. We then annotated the map with analytics regarding each asset and journey segment. I also completed a heuristic evaluation of the sell page using Nielsen Norman Group’s 10 general principles for interaction design

proposed solution

After evaluating all this data, we proposed these changes to the marketing emails:

  • Making each email look unique while maintaining the product’s branding
  • Explicitly stating how the product will solve the customers’ problems
  • Tightening up and front-loading the email copy

These were my proposed changes to the sell page:

  • adding customer reviews and social proof
  • shortening the overall length of the page
  • including language that explains how the product will solve customers' problems above the fold
  • reducing the number of items in the main navigation
  • adding 2 CTA’s above the fold (originally there were none)
  • using a persistent navigation that is always visible

results

The marketing materials and sales page were redesigned and launched. Marketing started November 2019 and will end the beginning of February 2020. There are several metrics that we are tracking to determine our success with the redesign. One metric we are tracking is total number of product sales. With only a third of the way into the marketing window, the redesign has an average of 8.9 sales per day, while the previous version had an average of 6 sales per day. This is a 67% improvement in product sales. For a $297 product this should result in an increase of $75,500 additional sales dollars for this one launch. We tend to market this product several times per year; 5 times in 2019. If we continue this marketing pattern for next year, the redesign should result in an increase of $377,500 for 2020.

Main Navigation

skills

  • Quantitative data analysis
  • Paper prototype testing
  • Wireframing
  • Collaboration

summary

Analyze our customers use of our current navigation and design a more useful and easy to use replacement.

problem

None of our customers were using the main navigation on our website. A recent heatmaps showed that out of 10,000 users, not one click went to the navigation. As a result, our users were using search as their primary means of locating products and information.

hypothesis

Working with the design and product manager, I hypothesized there were several reasons why our users were not using the main navigation: the link text was too specific, confusing, and there were too many items (11 links).

data to the rescue

I analyzed the search term data from the website from Q1 2019. The results showed that the majority (44.8%) of searches are for a specific author name. The next highest was topic (32.7%), followed by product title (13.1%).

proposed solution

Working with the design and product manager, we took all this data and created a new navigation that focused on the search terms our customers used. After two sets of in-person paper prototype tests, discussions with internal product teams, and several rounds of revisions, we finalized the wireframes for the new navigation.

The designs are complete and we are currently in the process of implementing this new navigation. It is scheduled for launch early 2020.

Product Categories

skills

  • Quantitative data analysis
  • Collaboration

summary

Determine new categories for all 8.5k products on an e-commerce site.

problem

The current product categories on our site are oddly specific, confusing, and are not industry-standard for our niche.

data to the rescue

There were several sets of data that was used to compile our new categories. First, we used the search term data from the website. The second highest (32.7%) type of search term used by our customers (Q1 2019) was topic, such as: mindfulness, meditation, yoga, etc. We then ranked the topic search terms our customers were using.

This clearly showed what topics our customers are most interested in. The second set of data we considered were the categories used by our direct competitors: Amazon, Hay House, Llewellyn, Inner Traditions, Shift Networks. The third set of data we considered were the number of products we have in the initial list of categories. There needed to be a sufficient amount of products in each category.

proposed solution

After considering these three sets of data, we agreed on a list of 14 parent categories and 20 child categories. Several different teams then went through our entire catalog of 8.5k products and recategorized them.

results

This newly recategorized product catalog has been uploaded to our under-construction site. The website is scheduled for launch early 2020.
