Information Architecture Redesign (Ecommerce Navigation)

  • Role: Lead UX Researcher
  • Scope: Ecommerce information architecture and primary navigation
  • Methods: Quantitative analysis, card sorting, usability testing
  • Impact: Increased navigation engagement, revenue lift, and improved UX scores

Context

Customers were struggling to find products through the primary navigation on the ecommerce site, defaulting instead to search as their main discovery method. While search provided access to products, it forced users into large result sets that required additional filtering, creating unnecessary friction early in the shopping journey.

I led a research-driven effort to evaluate how customers were using (and not using) the navigation and to redesign the information architecture to better align with customer mental models and shopping behaviors.

Problem Definition

Behavioral analytics revealed a critical usability failure:

  • Homepage click-map data showed 0% interaction with the main navigation across ~10,000 users
  • Customers overwhelmingly relied on search to locate products and information
  • The navigation contained 11 highly specific, ambiguous labels, making it difficult to scan or understand

Hypothesis: The navigation structure and labeling did not reflect how customers conceptualized the catalog, leading to avoidance and overreliance on search.

Research & Insights

I combined quantitative and qualitative methods to understand customer intent and mental models:

  • Search log analysis: The majority of searches fell into three categories:
    • Author name
    • Topic of interest
    • Product title
  • Gap analysis: These high-intent categories were not clearly represented in the existing navigation
  • Card sorting: Validated how customers grouped content and expected to find products
  • Usability testing: Identified confusion around labels and depth of navigation

These insights indicated that customers were not failing to explore, they were failing to recognize the navigation as useful.

Design & Collaboration

Partnering closely with Digital Design and Product Management, I translated research findings into a new navigation model that prioritized customer language and discovery patterns.

  • Designed a revised IA (information architecture) focused on top search intents and clearer category groupings
  • Iterated through multiple rounds of wireframes and interaction models
  • Facilitated two rounds of in-person paper prototype testing to validate comprehension and findability
  • Incorporated feedback from internal stakeholders while preserving research-backed decisions

Outcomes & Impact

Post-launch data demonstrated significant improvements across engagement, revenue, and experience quality:

  • Navigation engagement: ~12% of homepage clicks interacted with navigation dropdowns (up from 0%)
  • Revenue impact: Strategic product placement within navigation dropdowns drove purchase increases ranging from high double-digit to low four-digit percentage gains, depending on category
  • Experience metrics:
    • System Usability Scale (SUS) increased by an average of 0.94 points (5-point Likert scale)
    • Likelihood-to-repurchase increased by 0.85 points (5-point Likert scale)

While multiple factors can influence UX metrics, only two major structural changes occurred during this period (the new navigation and a stepped checkout), suggesting the navigation redesign was a meaningful contributor to these gains.

Lead Researcher Contribution

  • Identified a critical IA (information architecture) failure using behavioral analytics
  • Led mixed-methods research to uncover customer mental models
  • Translated insights into a scalable navigation strategy
  • Influenced product and design decisions through evidence-based recommendations
  • Measured impact using engagement, revenue, and experience metric
